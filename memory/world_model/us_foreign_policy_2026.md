# U.S. Foreign Policy — Trump Administration 2026
**As of: March 2, 2026**

## Core Posture
- Transactional, America-First; bilateral deals over multilateral institutions
- Aggressive use of economic coercion (sanctions, tariffs, investment restrictions)
- Military interventionism when perceived as low-cost (Venezuela, Iran strikes)
- Pressure on allies to increase defense spending
- Skeptical of prolonged Ukraine commitment; seeking negotiated end

## Key Actions Taken (2025–2026)
- **Iran/Middle East:** Participated in ~2,000 combined U.S.–Israeli strikes; Khamenei dead; now managing escalation + diplomacy (Oman channel)
- **Venezuela:** Military capture of Maduro (Jan 3, 2026); managing transition
- **Ukraine:** Mediating Geneva talks; backing security guarantees but pressuring Kyiv to negotiate
- **Middle East:** Backed Israel throughout Gaza war; Lebanon ceasefire over; Hormuz standoff active
- **DRC/Rwanda:** Brokered Washington peace deal; pursuing minerals agreements
- **China:** Trade war truce (Oct 2025); reciprocal visits planned
- **Syria:** Welcomed al-Sharaa; lifted sanctions
- **Latin America:** Cartel FTO designations; Colombia president sanctioned; Cuba back on SST list
- **India–Pakistan:** Brokered ceasefire

## Active Crisis Management (March 2, 2026)
- **Hormuz:** U.S. carrier groups contesting IRGC closure declaration — most acute military standoff
- **Qatar/Al Udeid:** Defending U.S. base under Iranian attack
- **Lebanon:** Supporting Israeli operations; monitoring Hezbollah escalation
- **Oman back-channel:** Trump confirmed openness to Iran de-escalation talks
- **Qatar LNG:** U.S. has interest in protecting Qatar; allied LNG crisis is strategic liability

## Legal/Constitutional Constraints
- Supreme Court struck down IEEPA-based tariff authority
- Administration pursuing alternative trade law mechanisms
- Ongoing legal battles over executive power in trade/sanctions

## Bandwidth Risk
- Simultaneous crises: Iran war, Venezuela transition, Ukraine negotiations, China trade management
- Risk of strategic distraction and policy incoherence
- Ukraine negotiations may stall as Middle East consumes attention
